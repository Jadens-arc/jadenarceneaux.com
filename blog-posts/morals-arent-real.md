Is someone who doesn't lie, cheat, or murder better than someone who does.

Let's back up and really uncover why people do things and what happens in someones head when they make a decision. 

## Triple Beam Balance
People love to say they have all sorts of reasons and rationals as to why they do or don't do things but I think we can distil it all down to a balancing act. Every decision we make is really an attempt to balance pain, pleasure, and risk. That's it.

### Example
Say you mess up at work but there's an opportunity to lie about it and get away with it. Some people might say "I don't lie, lying is bad, I won't do that", which is a reasonable response to that situation. But what that really boils down to is them balancing pain and risk. Owning up to their mistakes feels bad, but what feels worse for them is lying. Even if not tangibly, there is some psychological barrier that's preventing them from doing that and overcoming that barrier is harder than just owning up to it. Sometimes it's as simple as "that's just not who I want to be or what I think is right". The emotional scales in their head tipped toward the side of truth.

On the flip side let's look at the person who does the opposite. They lie. They feel like the risk of getting caught and the emotional pain of not telling the truth is lower than the pain of dealing with the truth, so they lie. Maybe it was their upbringing, maybe lying wasn't punished as much or nearly as much of a source of shame as the other person. But regardless they do it.

I think when you frame it like this you realize something eye opening. Both people are the same machine. Both people are unconsciously doing the exact same calculation just with different initial data, different weights on their scales. When you look at it procedurally, they're both performing exactly as they should. They are making logical decisions based on previous experience and data. Maybe there's something to be said that these two people aren't necessarily compatible in the same circles or even in the same society. But there's nothing broken about either of them they are both rational human beings.

When you look at things like this behavior becomes simple. "Why do people murder" because emotionally it was easier to murder than to not. That's not crazy, that's rational decision making. If you want to go further than that, it's just a matter of seeing what built those scales in their head. What kind of environment did they grow up in, did they experience trauma, do they have an physiological conditions. But at the end of they day, all your doing is recreating how the weights were stacked.

What even more interesting about this is the fact that people may even consciously know what they're doing is wrong, counterproductive, or harmful to those around them. But really what it comes down to are those underlying scales.

And sometimes the scales are wrong. Sometimes the pain you experience from doing something isn't fixed. Say you lie and then every time you see the person you lied to that pain only grows. Or we ponder on our past decisions and steadily those thoughts evoke negative emotions with in us that only seem to grow. The pain has grown but we've already made the decision. The scales have tipped in the opposite direction and there is nothing we can do with it. That's regret.

## My Thoughts

For me personally, I don't really think of people as good or bad. I think everybody with the mental capacity to do so is making rational decisions. I think in truth, everyone is doing what they feel is right. It's made it easier to empathize with people and truly understand where they're coming from and why they do the things they do, not just hand-wave-it-away with a "they're crazy" or "they're their own person, no one knows why they did that".
